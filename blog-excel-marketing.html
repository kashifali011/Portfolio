Using Excel to Optimise Your Marketing Campaigns Like a Pro

✍️ Kashif Ali 📅 September 2024 ⏱ 6 min read

Marketers love talking about expensive analytics platforms and AI-powered dashboards. But after years of managing campaigns for both a US-based brand and an Amazon e-commerce operation, I keep coming back to the same tool that most people already have on their laptop: Microsoft Excel.

Used properly, Excel is one of the most powerful marketing analysis tools available — and it's almost universally ignored beyond basic spreadsheet use. In this article, I'll show you the five Excel techniques I use to analyse campaign performance, spot opportunities, and make data-driven decisions that actually move the needle.

"Excel's flexibility and functionality allow you to track every aspect of your digital marketing campaign — from social media metrics and SEO performance to ad spend and ROI." — A tool most marketers massively underuse

Why Excel Still Wins in 2025

Before diving into the techniques, let's address the obvious question: why Excel when you have Google Analytics, Meta Ads Manager, and Amazon's reporting dashboard? The answer is that none of these platforms talk to each other. Excel is the one place where you can combine data from every source into a single view and do analysis that no single platform can do alone.

Every reporting platform gives you its own siloed view of performance. Excel lets you see the complete picture — spend, reach, conversions, revenue, and ROI — all in one place, compared across channels, across time periods, the way you want to see it.

Tip 1: Pivot Tables — Turn Raw Data into Instant Insights

1 If you're not using pivot tables for campaign analysis, you're doing 10x more work than necessary. A pivot table can transform thousands of rows of raw ad data into a clear, interactive performance summary in under 60 seconds.

Here's how I use pivot tables for campaign reporting:

  1. 1
    Export your campaign data from Meta Ads Manager, Google Ads, or Amazon into a single Excel sheet — one row per ad set or campaign
  2. 2
    Click Insert → PivotTable and select your data range
  3. 3
    Drag Campaign Name to Rows, and Spend, Impressions, Clicks, Conversions to Values
  4. 4
    Add a calculated field for CTR = Clicks/Impressions and ROAS = Revenue/Spend
  5. 5
    Sort by ROAS descending — immediately see your best and worst performing campaigns
💡 Key Insight

Always create CTR, CPC, CVR, and ROAS as calculated fields inside the pivot table — never import pre-calculated metrics from ad platforms. This ensures accuracy when your filters change and the numbers automatically recalculate.

Tip 2: VLOOKUP and INDEX-MATCH — Connect Data Across Sheets

2 One of the most common problems marketers face is data living in different places: sales data in one sheet, ad spend in another, website traffic in a third. VLOOKUP (and the more powerful INDEX-MATCH combination) lets you pull data from one sheet into another using a common identifier — like a campaign name, product SKU, or date.

A real use case from my work: I had Amazon sales data in one sheet and PPC spend data in another. I needed to calculate true ROAS per ASIN. Using INDEX-MATCH, I could pull the exact revenue figure for each ASIN from the sales sheet into my spend sheet without copying and pasting a single cell.

/* INDEX-MATCH: More powerful than VLOOKUP */ =INDEX(SalesSheet!C:C, MATCH(A2, SalesSheet!A:A, 0)) /* VLOOKUP: Simpler but column-position dependent */ =VLOOKUP(A2, SalesSheet!A:D, 3, FALSE) /* Calculate ROAS in the same row */ =INDEX(Revenue!B:B,MATCH(A2,Revenue!A:A,0)) / B2

The INDEX-MATCH combination is preferred over VLOOKUP for two reasons: it works when your lookup column is not the leftmost column, and it doesn't break when you insert or delete columns in your source data.

Tip 3: Track the Metrics That Actually Matter

3 Most marketers track too many metrics and make decisions based on the wrong ones. Here are the core metrics I track for every campaign type, and why each one matters:

CTR
Click-Through Rate
Measures ad/content relevance and creative effectiveness
CVR
Conversion Rate
Measures landing page & offer quality after the click
ROAS
Return on Ad Spend
Revenue generated per unit of ad spend — the bottom line
CPA
Cost Per Acquisition
How much it costs to acquire one customer or conversion
ACOS
Ad Cost of Sales
Amazon-specific: ad spend as % of attributed sales revenue
LTV
Lifetime Value
Total expected revenue from a customer over their relationship

Tip 4: Build a Campaign Dashboard with Conditional Formatting

4 Raw numbers are hard to read at a glance. Conditional formatting turns your spreadsheet into a visual dashboard that lets you instantly spot problems and opportunities without reading every cell.

My standard campaign dashboard setup:

  • Green-Yellow-Red scale on ROAS — green for high performers, red for campaigns bleeding budget
  • Data bars on Spend — visualise budget distribution across campaigns at a glance
  • Icon sets on CTR — up/down arrows immediately show which creatives are trending
  • Highlight duplicate values — catch data errors before they influence decisions
🎨 Setup in 30 Seconds

Select your ROAS column → Home → Conditional Formatting → Color Scales → Choose Green-Yellow-Red. Instantly, your highest ROAS values turn green and lowest turn red. Apply to CTR and Conversion Rate columns for a complete visual dashboard with no additional tools needed.

Tip 5: Automate Repetitive Reports with Macros

5 If you find yourself doing the same Excel tasks every week — importing data, formatting a report, copying totals to a summary sheet — you should be running a macro instead of doing it manually.

A macro is simply a recorded sequence of Excel actions that can be replayed with one click. I use macros to automate our weekly performance reporting process:

  1. Record the macro — Developer tab → Record Macro, then perform your usual formatting and calculation steps
  2. Assign to a button — Add a button to your sheet and assign the macro to it
  3. One-click reports — Every week, paste in new raw data, click the button, and your formatted report is ready in seconds

What used to take me 45 minutes of manual formatting every week now takes about 3 minutes. That's time that goes back into actual analysis and campaign optimisation.

The goal isn't to be good at Excel — it's to spend less time on Excel so you can spend more time on strategy. Automation is how you get there.

Putting It All Together: A Weekly Marketing Analysis Workflow

Here is the exact weekly workflow I follow using Excel to manage marketing performance:

  1. 1
    Monday: Export data from all ad platforms (Meta, Google, Amazon) and paste into the master Excel sheet
  2. 2
    Monday: Run the formatting macro — all conditional formatting and calculated fields update automatically
  3. 3
    Monday: Refresh pivot tables — immediately see which campaigns are performing above and below target
  4. 4
    Tuesday: Identify top 3 opportunities (scale winners) and top 3 problems (pause or fix underperformers)
  5. 5
    Wednesday: Implement changes in ad platforms based on Excel analysis
  6. 6
    Friday: Quick mid-week check on key metrics — anything that needs urgent attention before the weekend

This system keeps me consistently informed without spending hours in spreadsheets. The key is having a well-structured master sheet that updates quickly with new data, so the analysis time is minimal and the decision-making time is maximised.

Conclusion: Data is Only Valuable When You Act on It

Excel is not a substitute for good marketing instincts — it's the tool that validates or challenges those instincts with real data. The marketers who consistently outperform their peers are not the ones using the most expensive tools; they're the ones who look at their numbers honestly and make disciplined decisions based on what the data tells them.

Start with one technique from this article — even just setting up a pivot table for your current campaigns — and see how quickly it changes the way you think about your marketing performance. Once you see what the data is telling you, you'll never go back to managing campaigns by gut feel alone.
